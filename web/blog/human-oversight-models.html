Oversight architectures · AI agents

Where does the human sit?

Four ways to wire a person into an automated decision loop. The first three describe where the human is positioned. The fourth, human in control, is different in kind: it's an operating model that defines who holds final decision rights, regardless of who does the work.

AI / agent Human Authority Stop / veto

Human-in-the-Loop

Human gates every action

Nothing executes without a person. The AI proposes, the human approves, then the action happens. Maximum control, minimum speed - the loop runs at human pace.

Human-on-the-Loop

Human supervises, can intervene

The loop runs by itself; a person watches. The human monitors from outside and steps in when something looks wrong. Fast, but intervention is reactive - after the fact.

Human-out-of-the-Loop

Full autonomy, no oversight

The system acts entirely on its own. No human sees the decisions in real time. Fastest of all - and the riskiest place to discover the system was wrong.

Human in Control

One authority · execution mode set by risk

An operating model, not a position. Agents may propose or execute - scoped by risk - while a human retains final decision rights: the standing authority to approve, redirect, or stop any agent action at any time.

The reference matrix

Two different questions are being answered here. The loop positions (in / on / out) answer "where does the human sit in the decision cycle?" Human in control answers "who holds final authority and accountability?" - and is compatible with any execution mode. An agent under human control may propose, execute, or both; what never moves is the decision rights. Under both the EU AI Act (Articles 14 & 26) and Australia's AI Ethics Principles (Principle 8), the choice of oversight model is itself a compliance decision - not just an operational preference. Note also the distinction between operator contestability (the right to stop or redirect an action) and affected-party contestability (the right of people impacted by an AI decision to challenge it after the fact). Glossary

Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)
An oversight pattern where a person sits inside the decision cycle and must approve each AI output before it takes effect. The system cannot act without explicit human sign-off.
Human-on-the-Loop (HOTL)
An oversight pattern where the system acts autonomously while a person monitors from outside the cycle, with the ability to intervene or override when needed.
Human-out-of-the-Loop (HOOTL)
Full autonomy: the system perceives, decides, and acts with no real-time human involvement or supervision.
Human in Control
An operating model for AI agents in which autonomous agents execute the work while a human retains final decision rights - the authority to approve, redirect, or stop any agent action at any time.
AI Agent
An AI system that can plan and carry out multi-step tasks toward a goal - calling tools, taking actions, and adapting along the way - rather than just answering a single prompt.
Autonomy
The degree to which a system can decide and act without human input. Higher autonomy means faster execution and less direct human involvement per action.
Decision Rights
The formal authority over what gets approved, changed, or cancelled. In a human-in-control model, decision rights stay with the person even when execution is delegated to agents.
Oversight
The ongoing ability to observe what a system is doing - its actions, reasoning, and outcomes - well enough to exercise judgment over it.
Escalation
A built-in route by which an agent hands a decision back to a human when it hits a threshold: ambiguity, risk, cost, or anything outside its mandate.
Override / Kill Switch
A guaranteed mechanism that lets a human halt or reverse an agent's actions immediately. Decision rights are only real if this works at any time.
Guardrails
Predefined limits on what an agent may do - budgets, scopes, forbidden actions - that enforce the human's intent even between check-ins.
Decision Loop (OODA)
The repeating cycle of observe → orient → decide → act that any acting system runs, from John Boyd's OODA framework. "In", "on", and "out of" the loop describe where the human sits relative to this cycle.
Meaningful Human Control
The standard, drawn from autonomous-weapons debates and echoed in the EU AI Act, that human oversight must be real: the overseer needs understanding, time, and exercisable authority - not just presence in the workflow.
Accountability
The assignment of responsibility for an agent's outcomes to a named human or role. Distinct from execution: an agent can do the work while a person owns the result.
Explainability
The degree to which an agent's actions and reasons can be inspected and understood by its overseer. Oversight without explainability is watching a black box blink.
Traceability / Audit Trail
Logged records of what an agent did, with what inputs, and why - the evidence base that makes accountability enforceable after the fact.
Automation Bias
The human tendency to over-trust automated outputs and under-scrutinize them. The reason a human "in the loop" can quietly become a rubber stamp.
Rubber-Stamping
Formal approval without genuine review. It satisfies the letter of human-in-the-loop while providing none of its protection - the most common failure of HITL in practice.
Risk Tiering
Matching the oversight mode to the stakes of each action: low-risk tasks run autonomously, high-risk actions require approval. The mechanism that lets one operating model span multiple loop positions.
Governance Debt
Control that exists on paper but not in practice - a veto no one can actually exercise in time, an owner who lacks visibility, a kill switch never tested.
Contestability
The right of a person significantly affected by an AI decision to challenge that decision through a timely, accessible process. Distinct from operator override: contestability belongs to the impacted party, not the person running the system. Enshrined in Australia's AI Ethics Principle 7 and implicit in GDPR Article 22. Requires audit trails, accessible recourse mechanisms, and human judgment at the point of challenge.
Affected Party
The person whose life, rights, or interests an AI decision touches - and who may be entirely different from the operator running the system. A loan applicant, a patient, a job candidate. The subject of contestability rights. Governance frameworks that only address the operator's control leave the affected party's interests unprotected.

Lineage & sources

The in/on/out-of-the-loop taxonomy predates modern AI agents. It was formalized in the autonomous-weapons debate and builds on Boyd's OODA decision cycle. "Human in control" extends this lineage from position to governance - a shift regulators have already made.

Human Rights Watch - Losing Humanity (2012)
The report by Bonnie Docherty that classified weapons as human-in-the-loop, human-on-the-loop, and human-out-of-the-loop by degree of human involvement. The origin of the three-part taxonomy. hrw.org
US DoD Directive 3000.09 (2012, upd. 2023)
US policy on autonomy in weapon systems; the parallel vocabulary of semi-autonomous, human-supervised, and fully autonomous systems, requiring "appropriate levels of human judgment over the use of force." congress.gov
EU AI Act - Articles 14 & 26(2)
Requires high-risk AI systems to be designed for effective human oversight - monitor, intervene, override, deactivate - with deployers assigning overseers who have real authority, competence, and support. The regulatory codification of human-in-control. dlapiper.com
ISO/IEC 22989
Defines graded automation levels from full operator control through assistance, partial, conditional, and high automation - the standards-body view of the same spectrum.
OODA Loop - John Boyd
Observe, orient, decide, act: the decision-cycle model from military strategy that gave "the loop" its name.
Agentic governance literature (2025–26)
Current work distinguishing decision ownership from outcome ownership, framing agent oversight as an operating-model and decision-rights problem rather than a workflow checkbox - the context in which "human in control" is emerging.
Australia's AI Ethics Principles - Dept. of Industry (2019, upd. Dec 2025)
Eight voluntary principles for responsible AI design, development and implementation. Principle 8 (Accountability) explicitly requires organisations to "consider the appropriate level of human control or oversight for the particular AI system or use case" - making the choice of oversight model a compliance decision, not just an operational one. Principle 7 (Contestability) adds a dimension absent from most oversight frameworks: the right of affected people to challenge AI outcomes after the fact, distinct from operator control rights. Principle 6 (Transparency and Explainability) and Principle 2 (Human-centred values) directly underpin the explainability and trust rows in the matrix above. The December 2025 update evolved the original 8 principles into a Guidance for AI Adoption with 6 essential practices. industry.gov.au
